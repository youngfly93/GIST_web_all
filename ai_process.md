Data Re-annotation and Pre-processing Methodologies for GIST-Related Omics Datasets

Data source retrieval and cohort eligibility

The initial step in this study involved the systematic retrieval of omics datasets related to gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GIST) from a range of sources, including GEO, ArrayExpress, iProX, cBioPortal, and ethically approved in-house data from the First Affiliated Hospital of Zhengzhou University (ZZU; ethics approval 2023-KY-0353-003). Eligibility for cohort inclusion required histopathologically confirmed GIST cases, the availability of quantifiable omics measurements, and accessible clinical annotations, with ambiguous diagnoses excluded unless subsequent verification was possible. Ultimately, 38 datasets encompassing seven omics platforms—mRNA, miRNA, circRNA, proteomics, phosphoproteomics, single-cell transcriptomics, and genomics—were integrated using standardized computational pipelines, Non-coding RNA interaction databases (miRTarBase, circAtlas, RNAInter) were additionally incorporated as reference resources but are not counted as independent expression datasets. A detailed log of all included and excluded datasets, along with eligibility documentation, is provided in Supplementary Table 3 to ensure traceability and methodological transparency.

**GIST diagnosis verification and TCGA cross-check**

Accurate verification of GIST diagnosis across integrated cohorts was achieved through cross-referencing metadata, primary publications, and detailed clinical tables associated with each dataset. This required careful examination of diagnostic statements, histopathological confirmation, and relevant case descriptions, ensuring that only authentic GIST cases were retained. **Omics-specific preprocessing**

Preprocessing protocols were tailored to each omics domain to maintain data integrity across the integrated GIST cohorts. For transcriptomics, Affymetrix microarrays (GSE56670, GSE21315, GSE132542, GSE17743, GSE20708, GSE225819, GSE51697, GSE8167) were normalized using the RMA algorithm (Irizarry et al., 2003), while processed expression matrices from Agilent and Illumina platforms supplied by data authors were used when raw data were unavailable, a standard practice in GEO-based data integration (Davis and Meltzer, 2007); RNA-seq data underwent log2-TPM transformation or variance-stabilizing procedures, with all features mapped to standardized HGNC gene symbols via EnsDb.Hsapiens.v75 and AnnotationDbi for cross-dataset comparability. For cross-cohort visualization, gene-wise Z-score normalization was applied solely for display purposes, following the approach of GEPIA2 (Tang et al., 2019), TIMER2.0 (Li et al., 2020), and BEST (Li et al., 2023); statistical testing was conducted within individual datasets using untransformed values. Proteomics and phosphoproteomics datasets were handled using published quantification matrices (PMID: 37995868; PMID: 30804049), with the original studies applying 1% FDR thresholds consistent with shotgun proteomics standards (Nesvizhskii, 2014), and a casewise approach for missing data, as sample-level missingness reached up to 63.1% in proteomics and approximately 90% in phosphoproteomic experiments. Single-cell transcriptomic datasets (GSE162115, GSE254762, in-house ZZU) were processed using Seurat v5 (Hao et al., 2024), incorporating QC filters (nFeature_RNA 500–6,000; percent.mt capped at 15%), doublet exclusion, and cell-type annotation covering 9–12 populations per dataset, while non-coding RNA records from miRTarBase v9, circAtlas 3.0, and RNAInter v4.0 were integrated as reference interaction databases.

Additionally, rigorous management of missing data and implementation of quality control metrics were central to maintaining dataset reliability across omics domains. In transcriptomics, 21 of 27 processed matrices exhibited no missing values, whereas GSE36087 presented 24.8% missingness and gene-level coverage spanned 784 to 88,750 features, reflecting broad heterogeneity in assay design. Proteomics datasets, such as Sun's Study, encompassed missing value rates up to 63.1%, while phosphoproteomics experiments reached approximately 90% missingness, necessitating the use of available-case analyses to minimize data loss. Stringent filtering was applied for single-cell data, with only cells passing defined thresholds—31,870, 55,429, and 60,437 cells retained in respective cohorts and nFeature_RNA between 500 and 6,000, with mitochondrial content limited to 15%—to mitigate technical artifacts. These strategies contributed to the robustness and comparability of the integrated data resources. Quality control summaries are presented in Supplementary Figure 2.

Clinical annotation alignment

Careful alignment of clinical annotations was performed through extensive manual curation and standardization of cohort-specific metadata across all included datasets. Central variables, such as imatinib response, NIH risk classification, and known driver mutation status, were assigned using a controlled vocabulary specifically designed for this study to facilitate reproducibility and downstream analytical consistency. Specifically, imatinib treatment response was recoded to a binary classification (R: responsive; NR: non-responsive), NIH risk stratification was harmonized to four categories (Very low, Low, Intermediate, High), and mutation status was classified as KIT, PDGFRA, or wild-type (WT). The availability of categorical clinical attributes was systematically documented per dataset in Supplementary Table 1, and a variable dictionary was established to enable consistent interpretation between cohorts, as recommended for multi-omics data integration. Whenever ambiguities or missing entries arose in key clinical categories, harmonization efforts were limited to categorical level assignment, foregoing transformation of underlying continuous variables to preserve original data fidelity—for instance, age dichotomization thresholds differ between transcriptomics (65 years) and proteomics/phosphoproteomics modules (60 years), reflecting conventions adopted in the respective original studies. The conventions used for all harmonized labels, combined with a summary of variable distribution and completeness for each dataset, have been comprehensively detailed in Supplementary Table 2 to support transparent methodological reporting.

Cross-cohort comparability

Turning to cross-cohort comparability, all statistical analyses were intentionally conducted within individual datasets to preserve data integrity and account for assay-specific variance. Established tests—including t-test, Wilcoxon rank-sum, Spearman correlation, receiver operating characteristic analysis, and Kaplan–Meier/log-rank methods—were selected to maintain methodological rigor and reproducibility across the diverse omics platforms. Visual comparability between cohorts was achieved by applying gene-wise Z-score normalization and presenting results in a standardized, side-by-side format, thereby facilitating interpretable cross-dataset evaluation without introducing additional transformation bias. This per-cohort analytical strategy is consistent with established multi-dataset resources including BEST, TIMER2.0, and GEPIA2.

Data objects and reproducibility

To support long-term analytical reproducibility and facilitate data sharing, all preprocessed datasets were systematically stored as versioned R objects that maintained linkage with curated clinical annotation tables. These R objects were supplemented by a suite of precomputed visualizations, including gene-gene correlation matrices and essential summary plots, to provide immediate insight into dataset structure and quality. Designed for transparency and downstream usability, the storage framework enables researchers to reproduce analytical workflows, inspect processing steps, and access cohort-level attributes efficiently. Furthermore, there is a planned public release of these curated data objects in the form of a dedicated R package, ensuring broad accessibility and enabling integration with widely used R-based omics analysis pipelines. Such infrastructure is critical for fostering transparent research practices, allowing external users to validate findings, perform independent analyses, and build upon the existing multi-omics resource for GIST and related studies.
